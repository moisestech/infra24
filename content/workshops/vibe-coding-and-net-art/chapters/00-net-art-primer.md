## What “net art” means in this workshop

**Net art** (often used interchangeably with **internet art**) is art that treats the **networked browser**—links, loading, scrolling, defaults, errors, and social platforms—as part of the medium, not as a neutral frame around “real” art somewhere else.

Museums and critics began using **“net art”** widely in the **mid-to-late 1990s** as artists moved from isolated experiments to work that assumed a live, public web. The exact first use of the phrase is debated; what matters for us is the **shift**: art that only makes sense **because** it is online.

- [Tate: Art term — Net art](https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/n/net-art) — short institutional definition.
- [Wikipedia: Internet art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_art) — broader survey and timeline (community-edited; good for orientation, not as a single authority).

This workshop is **practice-first**: you will build small browser sketches, not write a thesis. The links above are **spines** you can return to when you want context.

## Key vocabulary (we reuse these words on purpose)

| Term | Plain meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Browser as medium** | Layout, timing, links, and built-in UI are expressive—not invisible scaffolding. |
| **Vernacular web** | Everyday visual and interaction habits of the early personal web: GIFs, tiled backgrounds, “under construction,” guestbooks, amateur enthusiasm. |
| **Hypertext / hyperlink** | Text (or images) that **jump** to another place; the reader’s path **is** the story. |
| **Interface** | The surface where human attention meets software behavior (buttons, hovers, tabs, scroll). |
| **Glitch** | A visible break in smooth media or UI—sometimes accidental, sometimes staged as language. |
| **Single-serving site** | A website with one tight idea, often one screen, one joke, or one mood. |
| **Documentation** | How you capture what you built (screenshots, short screen recordings, README-style notes) so others—and future you—can read the work. |

## How you will learn here

You will move in **short loops**:

1. **Constrain** — pick a small rule (one screen, one interaction, HTML-only, etc.).
2. **Build** — make something ugly-on-purpose first; polish later.
3. **Document** — one paragraph plus one image or clip of what changed.
4. **Repeat** — next chapter, next constraint.

Each reader chapter maps to a **segment** of the live or hybrid session. Use the reader **before** class to preview, **during** class as a checklist, and **after** class to capture what you shipped.

## The ~25-minute assignment pattern

Most chapters assume about **25 minutes** of focused making (not counting breaks or group critique).

In that window you should be able to finish **one small artifact** you can point to:

- A **single HTML file** or one tight page.
- A **visible change** you can describe in two sentences.
- **Proof** you did it: a screenshot, a short clip, or a link you could hand to a peer.

If you only have **10 minutes**, shrink the prompt: fewer lines of copy, one color, one interaction. If you have **45 minutes**, add documentation and a second variation—but **do not skip** the “one clear deliverable” rule.

Later chapters spell out the assignment as **“25-minute assignment.”** This primer is the contract they all share.

## Artists to know (this arc) — external links

These are **starting points**, not exhaustive bibliographies. Read bios lightly, then look at **1–2 works** each.

- **[Olia Lialina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olia_Lialina)** — artist, writer, educator; central voice on the **vernacular web** and browsers as cultural space. Pair with [Art Teleportacia](http://art.teleportacia.org/) (her studio site).
- **[JODI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jodi_(artist_collective))** — pioneering duo often associated with **anti-interfaces** and deliberate friction in the browser.
- **[Rafaël Rozendaal](https://www.newrafael.com/)** — **single-serving** sites and minimal interaction as composition.
- **[Heath Bunting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heath_Bunting)** — early net projects and **networked systems** as art.
- **[Shu Lea Cheang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shu_Lea_Cheang)** — networked bodies, identity, and surveillance as artistic material.
- **[Cory Arcangel](https://www.coryarcangel.com/)** — **appropriation** and digital craft with a dry, conceptual edge.
- **[Petra Cortright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petra_Cortright)** — platform aesthetics and **self-image** online.
