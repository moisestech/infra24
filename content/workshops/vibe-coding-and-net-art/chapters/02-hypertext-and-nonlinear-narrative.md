This chapter is the first real **narrative and structure** lesson in the workshop.

A link is not only navigation. In net art, links, splits, fragments, and nonlinear movement can **create meaning**.

Rhizome’s *Net Art Anthology* presents Olia Lialina’s *My Boyfriend Came Back from the War* as a foundational 1996 work, widely cited for how clicking causes the frame to split into smaller frames and produce a nonlinear narrative through browser structure itself.

> **Hypertext turns movement into part of the narrative.**

## Chapter outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- understand hypertext as an artistic structure  
- distinguish linear narrative from nonlinear web narrative  
- recognize links, page splits, and fragments as compositional tools  
- connect hypertext thinking to foundational net art  
- build a short nonlinear web piece of your own  

## Why this matters

In a book, structure is usually fixed and sequential.

On the web, a narrative can:

- branch  
- loop  
- fragment  
- hide information  
- split into multiple zones  
- ask the viewer to choose a path  
- reveal meaning gradually across pages  

This makes the **link itself** part of the artwork.

## A working definition

For this course:

> **Hypertext is writing or structure that unfolds through links, jumps, fragments, or branching paths rather than only one fixed sequence.**

In net art, hypertext can feel narrative, poetic, disorienting, cinematic, intimate, fragmented, or interactive.

## Key idea 1 — A link is a cut

A hyperlink does not just move the viewer.

It can act like:

- a cut in film  
- a jump in thought  
- a fork in a path  
- a reveal  
- a refusal  
- a delay  

Clicking changes time and attention.

## Key idea 2 — Fragmentation creates feeling

A fragmented page can create uncertainty, tension, intimacy, silence, ambiguity, or suspense.

In Lialina’s *My Boyfriend Came Back from the War*, sparse text and black-and-white image fragments unfold as the browser frame keeps dividing with each click. That formal splitting is part of the emotional structure.

Reference: [Rhizome — Net Art Anthology: *My Boyfriend Came Back from the War*](https://anthology.rhizome.org/my-boyfriend-came-back-from-the-war)

## Key idea 3 — Nonlinearity does not mean chaos

A nonlinear work can still be intentional.

The artist can shape:

- pacing  
- repetition  
- what appears first  
- what stays hidden  
- how many branches exist  
- whether a link opens possibility or closes it down  

Nonlinearity is not randomness. It is designed movement.

## Key idea 4 — Pages can become scenes

In hypertext net art, each page or frame can feel like:

- a room  
- a shot  
- a note  
- a memory fragment  
- a mood container  
- a threshold  

Instead of one continuous narrative, the work can be made of many small scenes.

## Key idea 5 — Reading becomes navigation

In net art, reading often includes:

- clicking  
- choosing  
- waiting  
- noticing what changed  
- returning  
- comparing fragments  
- remembering previous screens  

Reading is no longer only textual. It becomes spatial and behavioral.

## Questions to ask when looking at hypertext art

- What changes when I click?  
- Does the work open outward or narrow inward?  
- How many possible paths exist?  
- Is the link a reveal, a delay, or a disruption?  
- Does fragmentation feel intimate or confusing?  
- Do pages feel like chapters, windows, or scenes?  
- Does the work reward returning or rereading?  

## Mini artifact — Hypertext Fragment

Make a short nonlinear browser piece with at least **2 linked pages**.

Choose one direction:

- a two-page emotional shift  
- a mini story that branches in two directions  
- a poetic webpage with one hidden continuation  
- a split narrative with fragments instead of full sentences  
- a page where the link changes the meaning of what came before  

Your goal is not to build a large site. Your goal is to make one small work where the link matters.

### Easy mode

Create 2 linked pages with a simple mood shift.

### Medium mode

Create 3 pages with intentional pacing, visual differences, and one meaningful branch.

### Advanced mode

Create a short fragmented narrative using multiple pages, image/text contrast, and structural decisions that shape emotional rhythm.

## Reflection

- What did the link do in your piece besides move the viewer?  
- Did your work feel more like a story, a poem, or a sequence of scenes?  
- What part of nonlinear structure felt most interesting: branching, hiding, splitting, or delaying?  
- How does hypertext change what it means to “read” an artwork?
