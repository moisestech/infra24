Online identity is not only who you are.

It is also what you post, save, hide, circulate, archive, and leave behind across platforms and devices.

> **In net art, identity is often staged, traced, filtered, or networked rather than simply expressed.**

## Why this matters

A self-portrait does not have to be a face.

It can be:

- a webcam performance
- a folder of screenshots
- a browser-history aesthetic
- a phone archive
- an avatar
- a feed layout
- a sequence of reposted fragments
- a set of captions and traces

In *Riccardo Uncut*, Eva and Franco Mattes turned the media inside a purchased phone into a public slideshow portrait, making digital residue itself into portrait material.

## Working definitions

For this workshop:

- **Networked self** means a self that exists through platforms, devices, interactions, and traces.
- **Presence** means how someone appears or is felt online.
- **Digital trace** means residue left behind by media, platforms, and devices.

That can include selfies, screenshots, webcam clips, chat fragments, captions, saved images, browser windows, emoji patterns, profile pictures, avatar worlds, and device archives.

## Key ideas

### 1) Self-presentation is structured

Online identity is shaped by interface options, camera defaults, profile formats, follower dynamics, and platform aesthetics.

### 2) Devices can become portraits

A phone, laptop, inbox, or folder tree can reveal routine, taste, relationships, and anxiety.

This does not mean a device *is* a person. It means a device can function as portrait surface.

### 3) Avatars are real artistic identities

An online self does not need to map back to a stable offline identity to matter.

LaTurbo Avedon frames this clearly as avatar-based authorship native to internet and virtual space.

### 4) Presence and surveillance can overlap

To be present online is often to be observable.

A networked self can be expressive, exposed, searchable, captured, stored, sold, and watched.

### 5) Online awkwardness is material

Awkward webcam framing, cheap overlays, odd timing, and platform-native habits are not noise to remove. They can be core material.

### 6) Networked selves are fragmented

Online identity is rarely singular. A person can appear differently across private folders, public feeds, comments, chats, and avatar worlds.

Fragmentation is not a bug. It can be the subject of the piece.

## Self-expression vs networked self

### Self-expression

- personal feeling
- direct statement
- intentional representation
- a clearer voice/image of self

### Networked self

- platform formatting
- distributed traces
- archives and devices
- fragmented identity
- audience and visibility
- data residue
- avatars and mediated presence

Neither is inherently better. They create different forms of portraiture.

## Questions to ask

- What counts as the self in this work?
- Is identity shown directly or indirectly?
- What platform or device logic shapes the portrait?
- Does the work feel intimate, exposed, staged, or distant?
- What traces are left visible?
- Is this about expression, surveillance, performance, or archive?
- Is online presence treated as unified or fragmented?

## Mini artifact

### Networked Self Study

Make a one-page browser experiment about identity, presence, or traces.

Choose one direction:

- a page built from screenshots/captions that reads as portrait
- a webcam-like or feed-like self-stage
- an imagined avatar identity
- a device archive or folder logic as self-description
- exposure/privacy/online residue as composition

Your goal is not to define your whole identity. Your goal is one browser study where networked selfhood matters.

### Easy mode

Create a simple page using one image, one text fragment, and one design choice that suggests a digital self.

### Medium mode

Use HTML + CSS to stage identity through multiple fragments, captions, or visual layers.

### Advanced mode

Create a browser-based identity study where traces, screenshots, platform aesthetics, or avatar logic are sequenced to produce portraiture, exposure, or ambiguity.

## Reflection

- What kind of self did your piece show: direct, fragmented, archived, avatar-based, or platform-shaped?
- Did the work feel more like portrait, performance, trace, or surveillance?
- What system shaped identity in your piece: webcam, phone, feed, folder, profile, or platform?
- How does online presence change what counts as self-portrait?
