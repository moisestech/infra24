If you followed [Interface, Glitch, and Disruption — JODI](anti-interface-jodi), you already treated the **interface as mood** and met **anti-interface** tactics. This chapter zooms in on **glitch as language**: how noise, overload, and awkward layout can carry humor, menace, critique, or instability *on purpose*—with **JODI** as the primary reference and **Mark Napier** as the hinge toward “what the browser *does*.”

## Glitch reads when intention shows

Glitch is easy to fake with randomness. It becomes **language** when a viewer senses a **relationship** between signal and noise:

- **Pattern + break:** repeat an element until it becomes a grid or stutter, then snap it once.  
- **Legibility at risk:** almost-readable lines invite projection; total noise shuts it down.  
- **Material honesty:** default fonts, raw edges, and visible seams can be part of the argument.

**Featured artist — JODI:** landmark work sits in **web and software glitches as aesthetics**—wrongness, overload, and misdirection that still obey an internal logic.

**Learning goal:** use **distortion intentionally** so the page feels expressive, not merely broken.

**Key concept:** glitch can signal **instability, overload, humor, or critique** depending on rhythm and framing.

## Build: glitch field

**Starter action:** distort **text or layout** until the page feels expressive—repeated symbols, negative space that fights the grid, overlapping blocks, clipped overflow, `transform`, aggressive `letter-spacing`.

**Artifact to submit:** `08-glitch-text.html` (or an equivalent URL) with a one-line mood caption.

**Stretch prompt:** animate one layer so the glitch gains **time** (opacity, transform, or filter on a slow loop)—keep one calm anchor (title, footnote, or plain paragraph) so viewers know the storm is governed.

**Reflection prompt:** *When does distortion become meaningful?*

## Why Mark Napier here

**Mark Napier** (Potatoland; projects such as Shredder and Riot) often foregrounds **what the browser is willing to do**—load, transform, scrape—rather than only what HTML *looks* like. After a static-first glitch field, Napier is the natural next step: from **surface distortion** toward **behavior as material**. Continue on disk to **[Browser behavior as art — Mark Napier](browser-behavior-as-art-mark-napier)**.

Later in the handbook sequence you will also revisit motion and responsiveness in **[Interaction, Motion, and Responsive Behavior](interaction-motion-and-responsive-behavior)**—useful when glitch moves from layout into **timed** and **stateful** behavior.
