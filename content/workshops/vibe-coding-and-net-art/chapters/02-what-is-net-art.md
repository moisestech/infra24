This chapter gives the **conceptual foundation** for the rest of the course. By the end you should understand what makes net art distinct, how the internet can function as **medium**, and why **browser, link, interface, and network** can be artistic material—not only distribution channels.

## Chapter outcomes

- Understand a **working definition** of net art  
- Distinguish **art on the internet** from art made through **internet logic**  
- Recognize the **browser**, **link**, **interface**, and **network** as materials  
- Name several **foundational tendencies** (with links for deeper reading)  
- Sketch a first **“web gesture”**—a tiny page that demonstrates one idea  

If you have not yet done the tools chapter, you can still read this in parallel—but when you build, you will move faster after [Getting Started with Vibecoding](getting-started-with-vibecoding).

## What Is Net Art?

Net art is **not** only art that happens to be posted on a website.

**Net art** is art that uses the **internet**, the **browser**, the **page**, the **link**, the **interface**, the **network**, or **digital systems** as part of the work **itself**.

That means:

- **Structure** matters (paths, frames, hypertext)  
- **Delivery** matters (loading, timing, defaults)  
- **Interaction** matters (click, hover, scroll, attention)  
- Sometimes the **website is the artwork**  
- Sometimes the **click** is part of the artwork  
- Sometimes **layout**, **glitch**, **live feed**, or **online behavior** is the artwork  

> **Net art treats the web as a medium, not just a container.**

## Why this chapter matters (and how it connects to vibecoding)

Many people first meet digital art as **images on social media**, portfolio pages, or **documentation** of a project. That is not the same as net art.

A sculpture can be **photographed** and uploaded. A painting can be **shown** on a site. A video can be **embedded**. Those uses often treat the web as **distribution**.

Net art asks a different question:

**What happens when the internet itself becomes the form, the material, or the stage?**

**Vibecoding** in this workshop means short, iterative loops in that medium: you **describe**, **build**, **test**, and **revise** small web experiences. This chapter names *what* you are building *toward* conceptually; later chapters give **techniques** and **artist lenses**.

## A working definition

For this course:

> **Net art is art that is made for, through, or with the internet and its structures.**

That can include:

- browser-based works  
- hyperlink narratives  
- interactive webpages  
- online or networked performance  
- glitch and anti-interface works  
- generative or live pieces on the web  
- participatory or data-aware works  
- art that uses **feeds**, **platform behavior**, or **systems** as material  

## Art online vs net art

| Art online (distribution-first) | Net art (web-native logic) |
| --- | --- |
| Documentation uploaded to a site | The work **depends** on the browser |
| Images posted to social feeds | **Links** are part of the structure |
| A portfolio “gallery” page | **Interaction** changes meaning |
| A video embed as promotion | The **page** is part of the composition |
| PDFs or promo pages | **Participation**, **data**, or **network** behavior is built in |

The internet might **show** art online; in net art it often **shapes** how the work works.

## Key idea 1 — The browser as medium

A browser is not only a window that shows content. It is also a **frame**, a **stage**, a **surface**, a **set of rules**, and a **behavior system** with built-in expectations.

Net artists work with:

- layout and rhythm  
- scrolling and loading  
- windows and tabs  
- embedded media  
- hover, click, timing  
- tension between **usability** and **disruption**  

You will go deeper in [The browser is a medium](the-browser-is-a-medium).

## Key idea 2 — The link as structure

Links are not only navigation. They can be **artistic devices**: redirect attention, fragment narrative, create suspense, enable **nonlinear** reading, or hide layers until the viewer chooses.

That is why **hypertext** matters so much to net art—and why a later chapter focuses on [hypertext and nonlinear narrative](hypertext-and-nonlinear-narrative).

## Key idea 3 — The interface as aesthetic

Buttons, dialogs, loading bars, tabs, and cursors are **not neutral**. They carry feeling: corporate, intimate, hostile, playful, broken, smooth.

Net art often treats **interface as mood and meaning**, not only as function.

## Key idea 4 — Network, presence, and participation

Some net art is about **who is connected**, who contributes, who is watched, who clicks, who leaves traces. **Identity**, **chat**, **comments**, **data**, and **presence** can be part of the work—not background noise.

## Net art principles in this course

As you move on, keep these phrases in mind:

- the browser as medium  
- the page as space  
- the link as structure  
- interaction as meaning  
- glitch as language  
- remix as method  
- networked identity and presence  
- systems as subject  
- liveness and time  
- publishing as part of the artwork  

## A short canon sampler (external links)

This is a **sampler**, not a complete history. Follow one link, skim one work, come back.

- **[Olia Lialina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olia_Lialina)** — browser narrative, frames, hypertext; see also [Art Teleportacia](http://art.teleportacia.org/).  
- **[JODI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jodi_(artist_collective))** — anti-interface, glitch, disruption.  
- **[Rafaël Rozendaal](https://www.newrafael.com/)** — focused browser works; websites as art objects.  
- **[Mark Napier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Napier_(artist))** — software behavior and browser experimentation.  
- **[Heath Bunting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heath_Bunting)** — systems, networks, infrastructure.  
- **[Shu Lea Cheang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shu_Lea_Cheang)** — identity, surveillance, networked bodies.  
- **[Eva and Franco Mattes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eva_and_Franco_Mattes)** — internet culture, performance, online traces.  
- **[Cory Arcangel](https://www.coryarcangel.com/)** — remix, software culture, appropriation.  
- **[Petra Cortright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petra_Cortright)** — platform aesthetics and digital self-image.  

Institutional context: [Tate — Net art](https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/n/net-art).

## Mini artifact — Web Gesture

Make a **one-page** experiment that shows **one net art idea**. It does not need to be polished; it needs to make **one** concept visible.

**Choose one direction:**

- a page where **one click** changes the tone  
- a page with **one meaningful link**  
- a page that feels like an **interface**, not a poster  
- a page that feels **intentionally broken**  
- a page that **stages a digital self**  

### Easy mode

A rough single file (CodePen or one HTML file) that demonstrates **one** idea.

### Medium mode

**HTML + CSS** so the idea feels **intentional** (typography, spacing, color).

### Advanced mode

Add **one** interaction or behavioral change (hover state, toggled class, simple script) that reinforces the concept—still small enough to finish in one sitting.

## Reflection

- What feels most **different** about net art compared to other art forms you know?  
- Which part of the web feels most interesting as **material**: page, link, interface, interaction, or network?  
- Which **artist tendency** are you most curious about so far?  
- What kind of **web-native** piece do you want to try next?
