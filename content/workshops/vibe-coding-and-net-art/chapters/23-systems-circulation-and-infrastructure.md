The internet is not only made of pages.

It is also made of:

- routes
- servers
- protocols
- platforms
- exchanges
- networks
- permissions
- access
- participation
- hidden rules

That means a net artwork can be about movement, inclusion, exclusion, exchange, and invisible conditions.

> **In net art, systems are not background. They can be the subject.**

## Why this matters

Most people see interface first: feed, page, button, message, image, profile.

Underneath those visible layers are systems that determine:

- who sees what
- how value moves
- how information circulates
- what is stored
- what is filtered
- what is tracked
- what is blocked

Systems-based net art makes those structures visible.

## Working definitions

For this workshop:

- **System** means a structured set of relationships, rules, or flows.
- **Circulation** means how information, images, value, or attention move through a network.
- **Infrastructure** means underlying technical and organizational structures that make digital life possible.

This can include communication networks, barter systems, platform logic, access routes, exchange maps, data paths, participation structures, protocol-like rules, and hidden dependencies.

## Key ideas

### 1) The interface is only the surface

A page can look simple while being shaped by routing, permissions, moderation, payment rails, storage dependencies, and platform defaults.

### 2) Networks can be artistic form

A net artwork does not always need a central image. It can be a route, map, sequence of exchanges, participatory structure, or communication chain.

### 3) Exchange can become artwork

Value online does not move only through money. It also moves through attention, visibility, trust, credit, access, and community participation.

### 4) Participation changes the system

Some works only complete when users join, contribute, decide, or alter paths. The piece becomes an environment with rules, not just a static object.

### 5) Infrastructure is political

Infrastructure decides who gets access, what becomes normal, what stays invisible, and what gets excluded.

### 6) Systems can be poetic too

A route can feel eerie, a barter network can feel utopian, a flow diagram can feel absurd, intimate, or tender.

## Image/page vs system/environment

### Image or page-focused work

- visible composition
- direct interface
- what is seen on screen
- localized interaction

### System or infrastructure-focused work

- routes and flows
- exchange
- participation structures
- hidden dependencies
- permissions and access
- network logic
- circulation mechanics

Neither is inherently better. They create different ways of reading digital art.

## Questions to ask

- What is circulating: information, value, access, attention, or people?
- What rules shape the work?
- What part of the system is visible, and what stays hidden?
- Who participates, and how?
- What gets exchanged?
- Does the work feel like map, platform, route, or protocol?
- What infrastructure is being made visible?

## Mini artifact

### System Map Study

Make a one-page browser experiment about hidden systems, flow, or exchange.

Choose one direction:

- map attention movement through a platform
- diagram barter/value/trade logic
- visualize hidden routes, permissions, or dependencies
- stage a fictional platform or exchange economy
- reveal invisible infrastructure behind a familiar interface

Your goal is not to build a full platform. Your goal is one browser study where system logic is the core material.

### Easy mode

Create a simple page that maps one hidden digital system using text, arrows, or zones.

### Medium mode

Use HTML + CSS to stage circulation, exchange, or flow across multiple linked or layered sections.

### Advanced mode

Create a browser-based systems study where participation, exchange, route logic, or hidden dependencies are intentionally structured as the artwork.

## Reflection

- What kind of system did your piece focus on: attention, trade, access, routing, platform logic, or participation?
- Did your work feel more like a map, platform, diagram, or fiction?
- What was visible in your system, and what stayed hidden?
- How does infrastructure change the way we think about net art?
