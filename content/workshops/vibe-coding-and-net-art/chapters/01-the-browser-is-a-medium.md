## The browser as medium

A browser does **not** just display content.

It **frames** it. It **crops** it. It **organizes** it. It **scrolls** it. It turns code into **visible space**. It creates expectations about where things go, how they load, and how people move through them.

That means the browser is not only a container—it is **part of the artwork**.

> **When net artists work with the web, they are often working with the behavior and limits of the browser itself.**

Official references on how the viewport works as visible space and how layout relates to it: [MDN — Viewport concepts](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/Viewport_concepts).

## Why this matters

If you make a poster and upload it to a website, the internet helps **distribute** the work.

If you make a work that depends on **frames**, **links**, **scrolling**, **hovering**, **page divisions**, **browser timing**, **layout shifts**, or **window behavior**, then the browser is doing more than hosting the work—it is helping **produce the form**.

This chapter pairs with slides on workshop promise, outcomes, and the first artist lens (**Lialina** / vernacular web, **JODI** / disruption, **Rozendaal** / page-as-object).

## Key idea 1 — The viewport is a stage

The **viewport** is the visible area of the page. You do not see the whole webpage at once—you see a **framed portion** of it.

That means every webpage is already a kind of **crop**, **window**, or **stage**.

Artists can use that deliberately by thinking about:

- what appears **first**  
- what sits **above or below the fold**  
- what is **hidden until scroll**  
- what feels **oversized** or **offscreen**  
- what enters **slowly** or **suddenly**  

## Key idea 2 — Layout is composition

Layout is not only organization—it is **composition**.

Where elements sit in relation to the viewport, each other, page flow, margins, and the edges of the **window** changes how the work **feels**.

A page can feel calm, tense, empty, crowded, controlled, broken, cinematic, corporate, or intimate.

## Key idea 3 — Scroll is movement

Scrolling is **not** neutral.

Scroll can create **delay**, **reveal**, **pacing**, **suspense**, **vertical narrative**, **accumulation**, **exhaustion**, or **drift**.

A long page can feel like a **descent**. A short page can feel **abrupt**. Hidden content can reward **persistence**.

## Key idea 4 — Frames and windows shape narrative

Early net art often used **frames**, divided windows, and nested structures to produce meaning.

**Olia Lialina’s** *My Boyfriend Came Back From the War* is a key example: the browser frame keeps **splitting** into smaller regions as the viewer clicks, turning page division itself into **emotional and narrative structure**. Context: [Rhizome editorial on the work](https://rhizome.org/editorial/2016/feb/29/olia-lialinas-my-boyfriend-came-back-from-the-war/).

That shows layout can **tell a story**, fragmentation can create **tension**, and the browser window can feel **cinematic**.

## Key idea 5 — Browser behavior can be aesthetic

The browser teaches users to expect readable text, stable navigation, usable buttons, predictable layout, and clear hierarchy.

Net artists often work **with** or **against** those expectations.

**JODI’s** early web works used basic HTML and visual disruption to turn the familiar web page into something **confusing**, **unstable**, or **hostile**. The Whitney describes JODI as among the first artists to create Web art—see [Whitney — JODI](https://www.whitney.org/collection/works/33115).

Browser behavior itself can be beautiful, broken, poetic, annoying, disorienting, or critical.

**Rafaël Rozendaal** is especially useful for teaching **browser-space composition**: single-purpose sites where the **page is the complete artwork** ([newrafael.com](https://www.newrafael.com/)).

## Browser-medium questions to ask

When making browser-based art, ask:

- What does the viewer see **first**?  
- What is **hidden** until they scroll?  
- Does the page feel **open** or **cramped**?  
- Does the layout **guide** them or **frustrate** them?  
- Is the page **stable** or **unstable**?  
- Is there one window, multiple zones, or a **fragmented** structure?  
- Does the piece **reward** attention?  
- Does **interaction** change the mood?

## Mini artifact — Browser Study

Make a **one-page** experiment that treats the **browser** as part of the artwork.

**Choose one direction:**

- a page that uses **large text and empty space** to control attention  
- a page that **hides** key content below the fold  
- a page **divided into visual zones**  
- a page that feels like an **interface**, not a poster  
- a page that **intentionally disrupts** the viewer’s expectations  

Your goal is **not** polish. Your goal is to make the **browser visible** as a medium.

### Easy mode

Create a simple page with **one strong layout decision**.

### Medium mode

Use **HTML + CSS** for viewport-aware composition, spacing, and scroll behavior.

### Advanced mode

Add **one** interaction, reveal, or structural shift that changes how the **browser frame** is experienced.

### Alternate 25-minute assignment (HTML-only statement)

If your instructor wants the original warm-up instead: one full-screen **statement page** in **HTML only**—a title, one paragraph, and a background that sets mood (no images)—plus one sentence describing the mood.

- Which browser element feels most interesting: **frame**, **scroll**, **layout**, **window**, or **interaction**?  
- Which artist from this chapter best demonstrates **browser logic** to you?  
- How might your own work change if you treat the **page** as part of the artwork?
