Hover states train **where the eye rests**. In the studio lineage of **Rafaël Rozendaal** and other artists who treat the browser as a pictorial room, a tiny interaction can flip the whole emotional read of a page: reveal, veil, recolor, or re-crop without a single paragraph of explanation.

On touch devices, pair `:hover` ideas with a tap-friendly fallback (`:focus-visible`, a visible control, or a first-tap “arm” state). If the meaning lives in the hover, **document that equivalence** in your README or statement so the work survives different hardware.

## Learning goals

- Choreograph **one primary gesture** (hover or equivalent) that changes meaning, not only decoration.
- Keep **motion legible**: short durations and clear easing unless slowness is the concept.
- Articulate **what the viewer earns** by lingering — one sentence you could read aloud in critique.

## Composition prompts

1. **Single focal plane** — One large field (color, image, or type) and one cursor-driven veil. Ask: does removing the veil feel like intimacy or exposure?
2. **Shared mood line** — Three hover zones each write to the same line of text or strip at the bottom of the viewport (your “subtitle as instrument”).
3. **Delayed read** — Use transition-delay so the first motion is subtle; a second threshold reveals the “real” layer. Document why the delay matters.

### Sketch prompt

Three zones; hovering each zone changes a shared “mood line” of text at the bottom. Keep motion under 300ms unless slow is the point.

## Critique vocabulary

- **Attention is material** — Did the piece respect the viewer’s time, or merely animate?
- **Choreography vs. gimmick** — Could you remove one effect and strengthen the idea?
- **Touch parity** — What is the non-hover reading, and is it still a complete work?
